Quick Summary

Jimmy Greaves was the most natural goalscorer English football has ever produced. With 357 goals in England's top division (still the all-time record), he possessed an instinctive finishing ability that seemed almost supernatural. Lightning-quick in the box, two-footed, and ice-cold under pressure, "Greavsie" was a poacher without equal. Tragically omitted from England's 1966 World Cup final team after injury, he remains the ultimate "what if" story, though his legend as the greatest English goalscorer is undisputed.

Player Overview

Jimmy Greaves was goalscoring personified. Where other strikers relied on power or height, Greaves possessed something rarer: pure instinct. He seemed to know where the ball would be before it arrived, gliding into positions with uncanny timing. At just 5'8" and slight of build, he relied on agility, acceleration, and anticipation rather than physicality. His finishing was clinical—either foot, from any angle, under any pressure. In the six-yard box, he was untouchable. His record speaks for itself: 357 goals in England's top division (still the all-time record), 44 goals in 57 England appearances, and hat-tricks that became routine. The cruel irony is that despite being England's greatest goalscorer, he was omitted from the 1966 World Cup final starting XI after injury, watching from the bench as Geoff Hurst wrote history.

1966 World Cup: Football's Greatest "What If"

Greaves was England's best goalscorer, but hepatitis and injury before the knockout stages led to one of football's cruelest decisions.

Greaves was England's best striker but suffered a leg gash vs France in the group stage. Geoff Hurst replaced him and scored the winner vs Argentina. Manager Alf Ramsey kept Hurst for the final despite Greaves being fit. Hurst scored a hat-trick in the final. Greaves watched from the bench. He never received a winner's medal until 2009 (FIFA retroactively awarded medals to all squad members). One of football's greatest tragedies.
